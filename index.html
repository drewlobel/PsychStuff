1. Ethical Guidelines

What are the 4 APA ethical standards for human research?
  • Informed Consent: Provide purpose, risks, and right to withdraw[cite: 5].
  • Maintain Confidentiality: Right to privacy; info only used for intended purpose[cite: 5].
  • Deception: Only if no alternative, benefits outweigh costs, and debriefing occurs ASAP[cite: 5].
  • Free of Harm: No physical or psychological danger[cite: 5].

2. Types of Neurons

Define Sensory, Motor, and Interneurons.
  • Sensory (Afferent): Transport messages to the CNS from sense organs[cite: 5, 1042].
  • Motor (Efferent): Transmit messages from the brain to effector cells (muscles/glands)[cite: 5, 1042].
  • Interneurons (Association): Connectors found mostly in the CNS; connect sensory to motor neurons[cite: 5, 1041].

What is the "Synapse" and how do neurons communicate?
Neurons do not touch physically. Communication is electrochemical[cite: 5, 1048]. The Synapse includes the pre-synaptic axonal terminals, the Synaptic Cleft (gap), and the post-synaptic dendrites[cite: 5, 1047].

4. The Neural Impulse

Compare Resting Potential vs. Action Potential.
  • Resting State: No stimulation; membrane semi-permeable; inside is more Negative (Polarized)[cite: 5, 1048].
  • Action Potential: Cell fired; membrane permeable; channels open; positive ions flow in; inside becomes more Positive (Depolarized)[cite: 5, 1048].
What are Refractory Periods?
  1. Absolute: Immediately after action potential; membrane is not excitable[cite: 5, 1049].
  2. Relative: Membrane is excitable, but requires a stronger-than-initial stimulus to fire[cite: 5, 1050].

5. The Nervous System Divisions

Break down the divisions of the Nervous System.
  • CNS: Brain and Spinal Cord; interprets and sends responses[cite: 5, 1041].
  • PNS: Connects CNS to the rest of the body[cite: 1033].
    • Somatic: Voluntary muscle activation[cite: 7].
    • Autonomic: Involuntary (organs, glands).
      • Sympathetic: Activates "Fight or Flight"[cite: 7].
      • Parasympathetic: Inhibits/Slows down (Homeostasis)[cite: 7].

6. Brain Structures & The Endocrine System

Identify the 3 major brain regions and their parts.
  • Hindbrain: Vital functions (Medulla, Pons, Cerebellum)[cite: 7].
  • Midbrain: Sensory/motor clusters; Reticular Formation begins here[cite: 7].
  • Forebrain: Thalamus, Hypothalamus, Cerebrum (Cortex), and Limbic System[cite: 7].
What is the function of the Corpus Callosum?
A band of white fibers that holds hemispheres together and serves as a communication bridge, allowing the brain to function as a single unit[cite: 5].
